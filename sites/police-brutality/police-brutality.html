Introduction

Police Brutality has been affecting the lives of many minority groups, Including Native Americans. When considering Police Brutality and it's affects on Native Americans as a whole Barbara Perry (2010) has explored the ideas of where ethno-violence stems from, which will be introduced in the next few paragraphs. Ethno-violence is the idea that distinguishes unwarranted hostile acts against people for their race, religion, ethnicity, etc. A closely related term is considered to be "Hate Crimes". South Dakotas Yankton Sioux Tribe is all too familiar with ethno-violence and hate crimes from police officers.


The Underlying Causes of Police Brutality

Ethno-violence has been dated back to the beginning of colonization. Barbara Perry (2010) believes that police brutality and many other contemporary issues Native Americans face today stems from social oppression. She believes that "It is systematic and structural in nature reflecting a network of norms, assumptions, behaviors, and policies which are connected in such a way as to reproduce the racialized and gendered hierarchies which characterize the society in question.". Young (1990) came up with five inter-related "faces of oppression" in which we might characterize the experiences of minority groups: Exploitation, Marginalization, Powerlessness, Cultural Imperialism, and Violence. Barbara Perry (2010) states that structural exclusion caused by social oppression leaves minority members vulnerable to systemic violence which may run the gamut from ethnocide to ethno-violence.


DAPL

What began as innocent protests turned into a nightmare for Native Americans fighting for clean water. In the references I've provided a clip from unicornriot.ninja showing a short preview of the police's illegal actions toward the Oceti Sakowin tribes protesting the Dakota Access Pipeline. DAPL is a 3.7 billion project that would transport oil, under ground, from North Dakota to a refinery near Chicago, Illinois. This pipeline would run through Standing Rock Sioux Tribes only access to clean water. When this pipeline was first introduced to The White House Barack Obama declined it, but after Donald Trump was elected he passed the bill to start building the pipeline. Protesters against DAPL worry that the pipeline will contaminate their only access to clean water from the Mississippi River and could potentially ruin Standing Rocks sacred native land. Activists against the pipeline have shown numerous times that they have been peaceful and unarmed while protesting, but that still hasn’t stopped the police from mistreating and abusing the people fighting for their right to clean water.The earliest formalized police bodies in North America were constituted as slave patrols, with the legitimate authority to seek and apprehend escaped slaves as a means of maintaining their servitude (Bass 2001; Greene, 2003). Even in the beginning of forming police bodies we can see that the underlying cause stems from racism.


How the Sioux Tribe is Responding

The Sioux Tribe and many others are still fighting for their right to clean water. The Sioux tribe continues to protest in the only way they believe is right; Unarmed and peaceful, even when the police officers continuously harass the protesters using military methods of tear gassing them and spraying them with water the temperature of below freezing. Many of the Natives in South Dakotas Standing Rock Yankton Tribe participate in rally's and marches fighting for their lives and protection from police officers using the phrase #NativeLivesMatter.
